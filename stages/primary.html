primary tumor

what is primary tumor

Primary brain tumors arise from the brain, spinal cord, and associated tissues—collectively known as the central nervous system—rather than originating from cancerous cells that travel from elsewhere in the body. They are categorized as either benign (noncancerous) or malignant (cancerous). Examples of tumors that most often originate in the brain include meningioma and glioma.

types of primary tumors

There are various types of primary brain tumors, which are classified based on the type of cells they originate from. Some common types of primary brain tumors include:

  • Gliomas

    These tumors originate from the glial cells, which are supportive cells in the brain. Gliomas include astrocytomas, oligodendrogliomas, and ependymomas.

  • Meningiomas

    These tumors originate from the meninges, which are the membranes that cover the brain and spinal cord.

  • Pituitary tumors

    These tumors originate from the pituitary gland, which is a small gland located at the base of the brain.

  • Schwannomas

    These tumors originate from Schwann cells, which are supportive cells that wrap around nerves.

  • Medulloblastomas

    These tumors originate from the cells in the cerebellum, which is the part of the brain that controls movement and coordination.

  • Craniopharyngiomas

    These tumors originate from remnants of the embryonic pituitary gland and can occur near the pituitary gland and hypothalamus.

  • Pineal gland tumors

    These tumors originate from the pineal gland, which is a small gland located in the brain that produces melatonin.

  • Primitive neuroectodermal tumors(PNETs)

    These are rare tumors that originate from primitive nerve cells in the brain and can occur in various parts of the brain.

causes of primary tumor

The exact cause of primary brain cancer is unknown. Studies have shown a link between exposure to high doses of ionizing radiation and an increased risk of brain cancer. Research into the causes of brain tumors currently includes investigating environmental factors, exposure to certain viruses, and the role of genes. For most patients, there is generally no obvious exposure or risk factor that can be linked to these tumors.
